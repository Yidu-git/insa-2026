# Introduction
**Networking** in *computer science* is the process of connecting two or more computing devices to enable the transmission and exchange of information and resources. It is the fundamental backbone the internet and most digital experiences.

---

# Computer Networks
A **Computer network** is a system of interconnected computing devices. A computer network can range from home to cloud level environments  that communicate and share resources and data with one another.

A computer network can be classified based on two properties:
- Geographical coverage
- Class

## **Types of computer networks**
Computer networks can be classified **6** types of networks based on their geographical network:
### 1. Personal Area Network (PAN)
A **Personal Area Network** covers a very small area (a few meters), it can connect a few devices. This includes networks hosted by phones for file sharing.
Examples:
- Bluetooth connections
- NFC
- Mobile Hotspot
- Xender

### 2. Local Area Network (LAN)
A **Local Area Network** covers a small area such as a room, building or a small campus. This includes WiFi networks. It is most used for local and secure communication within a relatively small location.
Examples:
- Ethernet
- WiFi
- Gaming tournaments

### 3. Campus Area Network (CAN)
A **Campus Area Network** covers a larger area by connecting multiple **LAN**s. This enables CANs to cover Universities and corporate campuses.
Examples: 
- Ethernet
- Fiber optics
- Collage Networks

### 4. Metropolitan Area Network (MAN)
A **Metropolitan Area Network** connects multiple networks in a metropolitan region(*city*). Its most commonly used to provide high-speed connectivity across a city.
Examples:
- City-wide ISP network
- Metro Ethernet
- Fiber Optics

### 5. Wide Area Network (WAN)
A **Wide Area Network** covers large geographical areas such as countries or continents.
Examples:
- Satellite links
- Internet
## **IP (Internet protocol)**
**Classful IP addressing** was an early method used to assign IP addresses and divide the *IPv4* addresses space into fixed classes. Each IP address was categorized on its leading bits and the class determined its network and host portions. Due to inefficient address utilization, this method was later replaced by CIDR, which uses flexible prefixes instead of fixed classes.
